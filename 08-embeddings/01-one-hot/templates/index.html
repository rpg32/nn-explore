One-Hot Encoding

The simplest way to turn words into numbers — and why it's not enough

How It Works

Each word gets a vector of length V (vocabulary size). All zeros except a single 1 at that word's position. It's a lookup table — "cat" is word #0, "dog" is word #1, etc. The network receives these number vectors as input.

Problem 1: No Similarity Information

In one-hot encoding, EVERY pair of different words is exactly the same distance apart. "Cat" and "dog" (both animals) are no closer than "cat" and "the" (completely unrelated).

Word 1Word 2RelationshipDistanceSame?

The problem: One-hot encoding contains ZERO information about what words mean or how they relate. The network must learn ALL relationships from scratch — it gets no hints that "cat" and "dog" are more similar than "cat" and "the".

Problem 2: Extreme Sparsity

For a real vocabulary, each word vector is almost entirely zeros:

The problem: At GPT-2 scale, each word is a 50,257-dimensional vector that is 99.998% zeros. This wastes memory and computation. Dense embeddings (Module 08.2) compress this to ~768 meaningful numbers.

Things to Try

  • Type "cat dog fish" — see three one-hot vectors. Each has a single 1 in a different position. They look nothing alike.
  • Type "big small" — opposites, but their vectors are just as different as any random pair.
  • Look at the distance table. Every single pair has distance 1.414 (sqrt(2)). The encoding tells you nothing about meaning.
  • Look at the sparsity bars. Our tiny 30-word vocab already has 97% zeros. Real language models have 50,000+ words — almost entirely zeros.
  • The fix (next module): Instead of a sparse vector with one 1, use a DENSE vector of ~128 learned numbers where similar words have similar vectors. That's an embedding.
