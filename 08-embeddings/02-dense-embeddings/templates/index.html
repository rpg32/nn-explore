Dense Embeddings

Meaning as geometry — similar words live close together in vector space

From One-Hot to Dense

Instead of a 30-dimensional sparse vector with one 1 (Module 08.1), each word gets a 2-dimensional dense vector of meaningful numbers. Words that appear in similar contexts end up with similar vectors.

Below, every word is plotted as a point. Click any word to see its nearest neighbors. Notice how animals cluster together, verbs cluster together, etc.

Real embeddings use 128-768 dimensions. We use 2D here so you can see it directly.

Word Arithmetic

Directions in embedding space encode relationships

The vector from "man" to "woman" encodes a gender direction. Apply that same direction to "king" and you get... "queen". Meaning is algebraic.

Things to Try

  • Click on "cat" in the map. Its nearest neighbors are dog, kitten, puppy — all animals. One-hot couldn't tell you this.
  • Click on "ran". Nearest: run, walked, jumped — all motion verbs. The embedding captures part-of-speech.
  • Look at the distance table. cat-dog = 0.42 vs cat-the = 3.39. Embeddings make similar words close and unrelated words far — the opposite of one-hot where everything was equal.
  • Try the analogies. "king - man + woman = queen" works because the gender direction is consistent. This kind of algebra emerges naturally from training (Module 08.3).
  • Notice the clusters. Animals (blue), verbs (red), adjectives (green) naturally group. Nobody told the embedding to organize this way — it emerges from word usage patterns.
