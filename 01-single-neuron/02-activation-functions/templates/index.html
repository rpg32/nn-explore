Activation Functions

Why a neuron needs more than just a weighted sum

In Module 01.1, our neuron computed a weighted sum and that was the output. But a weighted sum is just a linear function — a straight line. Stack a thousand linear neurons together and you still just get... a straight line.

output = activation( w₁ × x₁ + w₂ × x₂ + w₃ × x₃ + bias )

An activation function is applied after the weighted sum. It introduces a nonlinear bend that gives neural networks the ability to learn curves, boundaries, and complex patterns. Without it, no amount of layers would help.

Why Nonlinearity Matters

Red line: Two linear functions stacked. f(g(x)) = 3(2x+1) - 2 = 6x + 1
No matter how many linear layers you stack, the result is always a straight line.

Green line: A ReLU activation between them. f(relu(g(x)))
The activation creates a bend. Now we can learn nonlinear patterns. Stack enough of these bends and you can approximate any shape.

This is the entire reason activation functions exist. One simple nonlinear kink, repeated across millions of neurons, is what lets neural networks learn to recognize faces, translate languages, and generate text.

Things to Try

  • Drag the input slider slowly from -6 to +6. Watch how each function transforms the value differently.
  • Look at Sigmoid near x=0 vs x=5. Near 0 it's sensitive; at extremes it barely changes. This is the "vanishing gradient" problem we'll revisit later.
  • Compare ReLU and Step. ReLU passes positive values through proportionally; Step just says "on" or "off." Why might proportional be better for learning?
  • Look at the comparison table. At x=0, what does each function output? At x=-2? These differences shape how a network learns.
  • Think about the linearity graph. If you stacked 100 layers without activation, you'd still get a straight line. The bend is everything.
