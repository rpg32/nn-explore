Module 09 · Attention

The Bottleneck Problem

Why compressing an entire sentence into one vector loses information

The Encoder-Decoder Architecture (Pre-Attention)

Before attention existed, sequence-to-sequence tasks (like translation) used this approach:

Encoder (RNN): Read the entire input sentence word by word, producing a hidden state at each step. The final hidden state is a single vector summarizing the whole sentence.

Decoder (RNN): Take that single summary vector and generate the output sentence word by word.

The problem: everything the decoder knows about the input must pass through that one vector. It's a bottleneck.

The Squeeze

Watch what happens as we encode sentences of increasing length into the same fixed-size vector:

▼ squeeze into ▼
16 numbers

3 words into 16 numbers = ~5 numbers per word. Plenty of room.
30 words into 16 numbers = ~0.5 numbers per word. Massive information loss. Earlier words get overwritten.

Hidden Size

Information Loss by Sentence Length

The solution (Module 09.2): Instead of forcing everything through one vector, let the decoder look back at the hidden state from every step of the encoder. At each decoder step, it picks which encoder states are relevant. This is attention — and it eliminates the bottleneck entirely.

Things to Try

  • Compare "tiny" (3 words) to "very_long" (30+ words). Look at how each word's contribution to the final state changes. Early words in long sentences contribute almost nothing — they've been overwritten.
  • Look at the words/number ratio. At hidden size 16 with 30 words, you're trying to pack ~2 numbers of info per word into 0.5 numbers of space. Something has to give.
  • Try hidden size 8 vs 32. Bigger hidden states help, but the problem doesn't disappear — you just push the wall back. With 1000 words, even 256 dimensions isn't enough.
  • Notice that later words have bigger bars. The RNN's recent memory dominates. Early words fade. This is the vanishing gradient problem from Module 07, seen from the encoding side.
