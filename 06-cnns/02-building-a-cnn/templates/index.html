Building a CNN

From raw pixels through convolution, pooling, and stacking — the full pipeline

Watch an image flow through a complete CNN. At each stage, the spatial dimensions shrink while the number of feature maps grows. Early layers detect edges, deeper layers detect combinations of edges (corners, curves), and the final flattened vector feeds into an MLP for classification.

Things to Try

  • Circle: Conv1 shows 4 edge maps — each highlights the circle's boundary from a different angle. After pooling, the edges are still there but at half resolution. Conv2 combines edges into corners and curves.
  • Cross: The horizontal and vertical edge kernels each light up on different arms. Conv2's "Cross" detector responds strongly because it sees both edge types meeting.
  • Digit "7": H-Edge finds the top bar, V-Edge finds the downstroke. Conv2 combines these into shape fragments. This is how a CNN would start to recognize "7" vs "1" vs "4".
  • Compare Max vs Avg pooling. Max pooling preserves the strongest activation (sharp edges survive). Avg pooling smooths everything out (weaker signal).
  • Watch the shapes shrink: 28×28 → 26×26 → 13×13 → 11×11 → 5×5. By the end, a 28×28 image has been compressed into a small grid of high-level features.
  • Look at the parameter count. Conv layers have very few parameters (9 per kernel). The Dense layer at the end has the most — but it only takes the tiny flattened vector, not the full image. That's the efficiency of CNNs.
