Gradient Flow

Why gradients vanish in deep networks — and how ReLU fixes it

Gradients are a product of local derivatives. In a deep network with sigmoid activations, each local derivative is at most 0.25. Multiply enough of them together and the gradient vanishes:

Sigmoid: 0.25 × 0.25 × 0.25 × 0.25 × 0.25 × 0.25 × 0.25 × 0.25 = 0.0000153
ReLU:    1 × 1 × 1 × 1 × 1 × 1 × 1 × 1 = 1.0

This is why sigmoid fell out of favor for deep networks and ReLU became the default. Compare them side by side below.

Things to Try

  • Start at depth=2. Both sigmoid and ReLU look fine. Now drag to depth=8. Watch sigmoid's early layers turn dark red — the gradient is effectively zero. ReLU stays healthy.
  • Go to depth=10 with sigmoid. Layer 0's gradient is astronomically small. That layer will NEVER learn. This is why early deep learning was stuck at ~3 layers.
  • Increase weight scale to 2.0+ with sigmoid. Now gradients might EXPLODE instead of vanish — the product of large numbers grows exponentially. Neither extreme works.
  • Look at the depth sweep chart. Sigmoid's first-layer gradient drops exponentially with depth (straight line down on log scale). ReLU stays roughly flat.
  • Notice ReLU isn't perfect either. Some layers show zero gradient — those are "dead neurons" where ReLU output is 0. The gradient passes through as 0. This is a known tradeoff.
