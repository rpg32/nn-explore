Overfitting & Regularization

When your network memorizes noise instead of learning patterns — and how to stop it

The setup: 80 noisy training points (small, easy to memorize) and 200 cleaner test points (to check generalization). A network with 32+16 hidden neurons — deliberately too powerful for 80 points.

No Regularization: network is free to memorize every noise point
L2 (Weight Decay): loss += λ × Σw²  — penalizes large weights, keeps boundary smooth
Dropout: randomly zero out 50% of neurons each step — forces redundancy

Watch the test loss: when it starts rising while train loss keeps falling, that's overfitting.

Things to Try

  • Run with defaults (200 epochs). Look at the "No Reg" boundary — it's wildly complex, tracing every noise point. Now look at L2 and Dropout — much smoother. Check the test accuracy: regularized networks score higher on NEW data.
  • Watch the loss chart. The red dashed line (no-reg test loss) goes UP after a while — classic overfitting. The network is getting WORSE on new data even as it perfects the training data.
  • The "Gap" stat shows train_acc - test_acc. A large gap = overfitting. No-reg has the biggest gap. Regularized networks keep the gap small.
  • Toggle to "Show Test Points" — see how the overfit boundary makes wrong predictions on new data, especially near the noisy training points.
  • Crank epochs to 500. No-reg overfits even more. L2 and Dropout stay stable — they've found the "true" boundary.
  • Set L2 λ too high (0.1). Now it UNDERFITS — the penalty is so strong the network can't learn the curve at all. Too much regularization is also bad.
